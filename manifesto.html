Manifesto

When we forgot how to build for this land

I grew up watching my house being built. I was in 9th grade, sitting with a scale and a pencil, sketching what my room could look like before I knew what a floor plan was. I didn't know the word 'Fenestration' then. But I knew I wanted a window that faced east, because mornings at home always came with light and sound and smell all at once. That window mattered. Not because it looked good because it changed the experience of my room. If we see Qutb Minar, the stepwells of Gujarat, the havelis of Rajasthan, the mosques of Bijapur. We can see that those builders did not just structurally build with every surface. They carved figures telling stories. Jali screens filtering harsh afternoon light. Deep-cut stone eaves throwing shade where shade was needed. Bracketed columns carrying ornamentation that doubled as meaning. These weren't decorations layered on top of buildings; they were the buildings. Form, function, culture, and climate, all carved into the same stone.

Now drive through Gurugram, or any new Indian city. The skyline looks like it was designed by someone who had never stood under a June sun at 3pm in India. Glass curtain walls. Sealed envelopes. Buildings that could be anywhere Dubai, Singapore, some generic render that won't a prize nobody remembers. The climate is the same. The culture is the same. But the building has nothing to say about either. This shows how modernism, in spreading globally, mutated into a style stripped of the local intelligence that once gave architecture meaning. A post-modern critique emerged that said: architecture must be a language. It must speak to where it is. It must carry cultural memory, not erase it. That argument has been made in the West for fifty years. In India, we are still choosing to ignore it.

The problem is not just aesthetic. These buildings are climatically illiterate. A glass facade in a city that hits 45C in May isn't brave design; it's a decision outsourced to an HVAC system, outsourced to the power grid, outsourced to someone else's problem. Our ancestors understood passive cooling through thick masonry, shaded courtyards, narrow alleys that funnel wind, and jails that let a breeze pass while keeping the glare out. That was not primitive. That was intelligent. We replaced intelligence with image. And the cultural cost goes even deeper. I want to design differently. Not by copying old forms that would be nostalgia, not architecture. But by asking the same questions those builders asked: What does this climate demand? What does this material know? What does this community already carry in its body about how space works? What does this site remember? My practice will be built on the refusal to be mute in a land that has always had so much to say.
